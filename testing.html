Nitrogen Mustards

Molecular mechanisms of DNA alkylation in cancer chemotherapy

Molecular Structure & Reactivity

R-N(CH₂CH₂Cl)₂ → R-N⁺(CH₂CH₂)₂ + 2 Cl⁻
Aziridinium Ion Formation

Bifunctional Alkylating Agents

Nitrogen mustards contain two reactive chloroethyl groups (-CH₂CH₂Cl) attached to a central nitrogen atom. The tertiary amine structure facilitates intramolecular cyclization through nucleophilic displacement.

Electrophilic Reactivity

The spontaneous formation of highly strained aziridinium ions (three-membered rings) creates powerful electrophilic centers that readily attack nucleophilic sites in DNA, particularly the N7 position of guanine.

Step-by-Step Molecular Mechanism

1Aziridinium Ion Formation

Rate constant: k₁ = 2.1 × 10⁻⁴ s⁻¹ at pH 7.4, 37°C

The nitrogen's lone pair attacks the β-carbon of the chloroethyl group via an intramolecular SN2 mechanism, displacing chloride and forming a highly reactive aziridinium ion intermediate. The three-membered ring exhibits significant angle strain (~60° vs. tetrahedral 109.5°), making it exceptionally electrophilic.

R-N(CH₂CH₂Cl)₂ → R-N⁺-CH₂CH₂ + Cl⁻ \_______/ (aziridinium)

2DNA Nucleophilic Attack

Target specificity: N7-guanine (pKa ~3.5) > N3-adenine (pKa ~1.5)

The N7 position of guanine, being the most nucleophilic site in DNA due to its electron-rich lone pair and accessibility in the major groove, attacks the aziridinium carbon. This forms a covalent N7-alkylguanine monoadduct.

Guanine-N7: + R-N⁺-CH₂CH₂ → Guanine-N7-CH₂CH₂-N⁺-R

3Interstrand Cross-Link Formation

Optimal spacing: 8.9 Å between N7 atoms of opposing guanines

The second chloroethyl arm undergoes identical activation, forming another aziridinium ion that reacts with a guanine on the complementary DNA strand. This creates a covalent interstrand cross-link, predominantly at 5'-GNC-3' sequences.

5'-G-N-C-3' | | 3'-C-N-G-5' Cross-linked DNA
